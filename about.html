Why This Business Exists

Digital ABCs was built by someone who had to make every hour count.

This business did not come from a startup fantasy. It came from lived pressure, real constraints, and a refusal to accept that messy systems are just “how things are”. That is why the work is direct, practical, and honest about what helps and what does not.

Simple tools for self-understanding

We build self-quantification apps that become more valuable over time, revealing patterns and insights that only emerge through consistent tracking.

Our mission

To provide simple, beautiful tools for self-understanding. We build apps that grow more valuable over time, making it easy to track patterns and impossible to leave once you have months of personal history.

Our vision

A world where self-awareness is accessible to everyone. We envision a future where understanding your own patterns — moods, habits, relationships, health — is as natural as checking your email. Where data-driven self-knowledge empowers better decisions without feeling clinical or overwhelming.

The Short Version

Grit matters because constraints shape the work.

I am a First Nations, neurodivergent single mum who has spent a lot of time navigating systems that were never designed with people like me in mind. That changes how I look at business, software, and time.

I notice friction quickly. I notice waste. I notice the extra steps people tolerate because no one has stopped to ask whether they should exist at all. Digital ABCs grew out of that pattern recognition and out of sheer necessity.

The goal is not to sound impressive. The goal is to build useful things, tell the truth about where they are up to, and create work that supports a real life rather than consuming it.

That is why the business leans hard toward plain English, practical delivery, and honesty about limitations. If something is not ready, it is not ready. If a simpler fix will do the job, that is the fix worth recommending.

What we believe

Our core values guide every decision we make, from product design to data handling.

Clarity over complexity

We choose simple, understandable interfaces over feature bloat. Every screen should have one primary purpose.

Privacy as a right

Users trust us with intimate data. We never sell data, we use minimal collection, and we comply with Australian Privacy Principles.

Value before effort

Show users what they'll get before asking them to work. Demo data first, then logging. Value previews before paywalls.

Human insight, AI assistance

AI helps spot patterns, but users interpret meaning. We augment self-awareness, we don't replace it.

Data lock-in as moat

Users stay because their history is valuable, not because we hide export. We celebrate data accumulation openly.

Accessible by default

WCAG 2.1 Level AAA compliance isn't a nice-to-have, it's table stakes. Everyone deserves beautiful, usable tools.

What Clients Get

The business promise is simple.

Clients and beta users are not there to prop up a brand story. They should get something useful, clear expectations, and straight answers about risk, scope, and trade-offs.

Plain language

No jargon shield. If a solution cannot be explained clearly, it probably is not ready to be trusted.

Practical builds

The focus is on what will genuinely reduce load, improve a workflow, or create a usable product, not on building something bloated for the sake of it.

Straight-up status

Finished work, beta work, and future work are treated differently. That separation is part of the trust model.

Trish Eastcott

Founder, Digital ABCs

I'm a First Nations, neurodivergent single mum. I've got bipolar, CPTSD, and ADHD. If you've ever dealt with Centrelink while trying to juggle mental health and parenting, you already know what that's like.

Here's the thing about being neurodivergent: my brain sees gaps everywhere. Where most people see "this is just how it's done," I see the thing that should be easier but isn't. The tracker that doesn't exist. The tool that almost works but not quite. So I build them.

I'm completely self-taught — no computer science degree, no coding bootcamp. Just YouTube, Google, and stubborn persistence until things work. Everything I know about building software, I learned by doing it.

Digital ABCs started as tools I built for myself. Apps to track my moods, understand my patterns, and make sense of my own experience. Then I realised other people — especially other neurodivergent people — need these same tools. So I started sharing them.
